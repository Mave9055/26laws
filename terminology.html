Core Survival Concepts

Complex Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (CPTSD)

Clinical Definition: A diagnostic category for a trauma disorder following prolonged, repeated, or cumulative trauma where the victim has little to no chance of escape.

Peer Education / The Blueprint: A survival architecture. It's what happens when your nervous system is forced to adapt to a sustained state of terror, turning off your ability to trust, feel safe, or regulate emotion. You're not broken—your survival wiring is stuck in the 'on' position.

Takeaway: CPTSD is a functional description of your survival state, not a permanent diagnosis of your worth.

Emotional Dysregulation

Clinical Definition: The inability to modulate the intensity and duration of emotional responses, often resulting in sudden, disproportionate, or prolonged mood states (e.g., explosive anger or deep despair).

Peer Education / The Blueprint: A system failure. When the nervous system is chronically tuned to high-alert (hypervigilance), the brain's emotional brake pedal (prefrontal cortex) never develops properly. Your emotions are not "too much"; your regulatory system is under-resourced and easily overwhelmed.

Takeaway: Healing isn't about control; it's about building emotional resilience through nervous system repair.

Hypervigilance

Clinical Definition: An enhanced state of sensory sensitivity accompanied by an exaggerated intensity of behaviors whose purpose is to detect threat. It is a constant, exhausting feeling of being on guard.

Peer Education / The Blueprint: A necessary survival program. You were constantly scanning your environment for threats because danger was real and pervasive. This mechanism saved your life, but it has outlived its usefulness. Your task is to convince your body the threat is over.

Takeaway: You can't talk yourself out of hypervigilance; you have to teach your body safety.

Clinical Terminology & The Wounded Blueprint Glossary

The goal of this framework is to move emotional experiences from the moral filing cabinet ("I am bad") to the biological filing cabinet ("My wiring is overactive"). Understanding this precise language is the first step.

Allostatic Load
The cumulative "wear and tear" on the body and brain resulting from chronic, repeated stress. Holding onto unresolved rage (Law #24) contributes directly to allostatic load by keeping stress hormones elevated.
Amygdala
The brain's primary threat detection center. In a traumatized nervous system, the amygdala is hyperactive and constantly triggers false alarms, associating safe stimuli with danger (Law #1, Law #24).
Artificial Ventral Surrogate
A substance or behavior (e.g., alcohol, work, food, excessive exercise) used to chemically or behaviorally mimic the regulated state of the Ventral Vagal Complex. This is the neurobiological definition of addiction (Law #16).
Coherent Multiplicity (Internal Family Systems - IFS)
The state of integration where all the different "parts" of the self (protectors, exiles, firefighters) are recognized, valued, and brought into conscious collaboration, rather than being eliminated or suppressed (Law #25).
Co-Regulation
The process by which one nervous system is calmed and regulated by the presence of another, typically calmer, nervous system. This is a foundational human need, and its absence leads to dysregulation (Law #8, Law #18).
Dorsal Vagal Shutdown
A severe state of nervous system collapse (part of the Polyvagal Theory) characterized by feeling numb, dissociated, depressed, or emotionally flat. It is the body's last-resort defense mechanism (Law #14).
Emotional Flashback
A trauma response where the body and emotions react to a present stimulus as if the past traumatic event is happening again, without the conscious visual memory of the event (Law #7, Law #17).
Fawn Response (4 Fs)
A trauma survival strategy (one of the four Fs: fight, flight, freeze, fawn) where the individual attempts to prevent harm by appeasing, people-pleasing, or over-explaining to the threat source. Over-justifying boundaries is a form of this (Law #19).
Functional Hyperarousal
A state where chronic, high-level anxiety (hypervigilance) is channeled into high-stakes performance and productivity. This creates a state of functional stress that temporarily regulates internal chaos but leads to burnout (Law #5).
Institutional Betrayal
Harm perpetrated by institutions (e.g., healthcare, legal, religious systems) that an individual relies on for safety or well-being. This leads to profound distrust and avoidance of necessary systems (Law #13).
Learned Helplessness
A psychological state where an individual stops attempting to avoid painful or negative stimuli because they have learned, through repeated experience, that their actions are futile. Leads to passive resignation and non-compliance (Law #13).
Neuroception
Dr. Stephen Porges' term for the unconscious process by which the nervous system scans the environment (and other nervous systems) for cues of safety or danger, bypassing cognitive thought. Often overrides conscious intention (Law #1, Law #18).
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. This is the mechanism by which repeated trauma responses (like hypervigilance) become deep, entrenched "default highways" (Law #11).
Post-Traumatic Growth (PTG)
Fundamental, positive psychological change experienced as a result of the struggle with highly challenging life circumstances, such as trauma. It is an intentional shift in perspective and purpose (Law #26).
Prefrontal Cortex (PFC)
The area of the brain responsible for executive functions, including rational thought, linear memory, planning, and emotional regulation. This area is often functionally inhibited or "shut down" by stress hormones (Law #12).
Window of Tolerance
Dr. Daniel Siegel's concept describing the optimal zone of arousal where a person can function effectively, think clearly, and tolerate emotional distress without falling into states of hyperarousal (fight/flight) or hypoarousal (freeze/dissociation) (Law #11).
